Why I Lift

Weightlifting isn't just about aesthetics — it's about building a version of yourself that's stronger in every way. I started lifting seriously after returning from two years in Vietnam, and it quickly became one of the most grounding habits in my life.

The gym teaches patience, consistency, and discipline. You can't fake a heavy squat. You can't outsmart progressive overload. You just have to show up, put in the work, and trust the process.

Whether you're chasing a 500 lb deadlift or just trying to feel better in your daily life, lifting has something to offer everyone.

The barbell — simple, honest, unforgiving.

The Big Three Lifts

Powerlifting is built on three foundational movements. Master these and everything else follows.

  1. The Squat — The king of all exercises. Builds the entire lower body and core.
    • Back Squat — most common, barbell on upper back
    • Front Squat — barbell on front shoulders, more quad-dominant
    • Pause Squat — improves strength out of the hole
    • Box Squat — teaches proper depth and control
  2. The Bench Press — Upper body strength staple. Chest, shoulders, and triceps.
    • Flat Bench — classic horizontal press
    • Incline Bench — greater upper pec emphasis
    • Close Grip — tricep-dominant variation
    • Pause Bench — eliminates the bounce, builds true strength
  3. The Deadlift — The ultimate test of total body strength. Nothing else comes close.
    • Conventional — narrow stance, hands outside legs
    • Sumo — wide stance, more hip-dominant
    • Romanian (RDL) — hamstring focused, hip hinge
    • Deficit Deadlift — extended range of motion for more difficulty

Training Philosophies

There's more than one way to get strong. Here are the main schools of thought:

Powerlifting

Focuses on maximizing 1-rep maxes in squat, bench, and deadlift. Low reps, high intensity, long rest periods.

Hypertrophy

Building muscle size through higher volume (sets × reps). Typically 8–15 rep ranges with moderate weight.

Olympic Lifting

Snatch and clean & jerk — explosive, technical movements that demand strength, speed, and coordination.

General Strength

Programs like Starting Strength or StrongLifts. Progressive overload over months. Great for beginners.

Fueling the Work

Nutrition is 80% of the equation.

You can train perfectly and still spin your wheels if your nutrition is off. The fundamentals are simple but often ignored:

  • Protein: Aim for ~0.8–1g per pound of bodyweight daily. Chicken, beef, eggs, Greek yogurt.
  • Calories: Bulk (surplus) to gain muscle, cut (deficit) to lose fat. Don't try both at once.
  • Carbs: Your primary energy source. Don't fear them — time them around workouts.
  • Sleep: Technically not food, but 7–9 hours of sleep is when you actually grow.
  • Hydration: Half your bodyweight in oz of water per day minimum.
